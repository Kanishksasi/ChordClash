Music Theory Lessons

1. Major Chords

The foundation of music — bright, happy sounds built from three notes.

Formula: Root + Major 3rd (4 semitones) + Perfect 5th (7 semitones)

C Major C + E + G
G Major G + B + D
D Major D + F# + A

Sound: Bright, confident, happy. Used in pop, rock, and classical.
Tip: The gap between Root and 3rd is always 4 keys on a piano (counting black keys).

2. Minor Chords

Darker, more emotional versions of major chords — one note lowered by a semitone.

Formula: Root + Minor 3rd (3 semitones) + Perfect 5th (7 semitones)

A Minor A + C + E
E Minor E + G + B
D Minor D + F + A

Sound: Sad, mysterious, emotional. Common in ballads and metal.
Tip: Minor = Major with the middle note lowered by one semitone (one black key).

3. Power Chords

Two-note chords (root + 5th) beloved by rock and metal guitarists for their raw sound.

Formula: Root + Perfect 5th (7 semitones) — no 3rd!

E5 E + B
A5 A + E
G5 G + D

Sound: Heavy, neutral (neither major nor minor). Great for distorted guitar tones.
Tip: Because there's no 3rd, power chords work equally well as major or minor substitutes.

4. Chord Progressions

How chords move together to create musical phrases, tension, and resolution.

Common Progressions:

I–V–vi–IV C–G–Am–F (Pop standard — used in hundreds of hits)
I–IV–V G–C–D (Classic blues/rock foundation)
vi–IV–I–V Am–F–C–G (Emotional pop/ballad feel)

Why it matters: Progressions create the emotional arc of a song. Learning them helps you predict — and play — new songs faster.
Tip: Roman numerals (I, IV, V) refer to scale degrees, so the same progression works in any key.

5. 7th Chords & Extensions

Add a fourth note to triads for richer, more complex harmonic color.

Types:

Major 7th Major triad + Major 7th — e.g. Cmaj7: C E G B (dreamy, jazz)
Dominant 7th Major triad + Minor 7th — e.g. G7: G B D F (bluesy tension)
Minor 7th Minor triad + Minor 7th — e.g. Am7: A C E G (smooth, soulful)
Diminished 7th 3 minor 3rds stacked — e.g. Cdim7: C Eb Gb A (eerie, dramatic)

Tip: 7th chords are the gateway to jazz. Once comfortable with triads, start adding the 7th to your progressions.
